🎯 Overall strategy — professor's 5 directional rules

  1. Produce where the demand is. Local production, avoid cross-area transfers (transport & quota friction).
  2. Vertical R&D. Invest in own R&D for grade upgrades — do NOT buy Clevers licences even when cheap.
  3. Old-grade tech sold cheap. Demand for grade-1 stock is strong — price aggressively low to clear (esp. JAP-X-Std 76k overhang, USA-Y-Std 42k overhang).
  4. Premium pricing on new grades. When grade-2 lands, that's where margin extraction happens — DLx + premium.
  5. Cash > inventory. Match production to forecast tightly. Don't sit on stock hoping it sells.

Directional — not a hard rule. If a specific opportunity clearly beats local-only (e.g. Han River tender at $30/u), take it.

How the demand model works (click to expand)
For each line (area, product, grade): base_demand = MARKET_SIZE[area][product] × DLx_or_Std_share × (1 / num_competitors) price_factor = (cohort_price / our_price) ^ elasticity // ε > 1; cheaper = more demand ad_factor = bounded log(ad_spend / ad_optimum) // diminishing returns, capped 0.5–1.5 bc_factor = Business_Cycle_index / 100 // macro pull/drag demand = base_demand × price_factor × ad_factor × bc_factor sales = min(demand, starting_inventory + production) // stockout if demand > available end_inv = starting_inventory + production − sales

Lagged advertising carryover, competitor stockout redistribution, brand loyalty, and seasonality are ignored at this stage — fold them in once we have cohort P5 actuals.
